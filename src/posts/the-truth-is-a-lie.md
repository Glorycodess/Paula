In 1933, Franklin D. Roosevelt stood before a nation gripped by fear and said 'the only thing we have to fear is fear itself.' These words are older than me but they mean so much to me, because after living two decades on this earth I can now see why that statement was necessary

You see, my parents have been divorced since I was two years old, yet my childhood is filled with memories of visiting my dad almost every other weekend. Going over was always fun, but my favourite part was Daniel. Danny, as we would always call him, lived with his family near my dad's house and whenever I visited, him and I would always sit together to play. I was around seven to twelve years old and he was older. He was the most beautiful-looking boy I had ever seen in my life, and I had the biggest crush on him. Daniel seemed to like cool people, and this made me always act cool. I wanted him to like me back but I was also too shy to let him know.

One of my earliest memories of Danny was the day he taught me to ride a bicycle. He would also greet my dad so warmly that my dad genuinely liked him. I remember how we would talk about life and our futures, how we would be rich and how life would be good to us. But my time with Danny was always short because every Sunday night I had to go back to my mum's place to prepare for school on Monday.

All my friends in school knew about Danny. They knew how handsome he was, how he taught me to ride a bike, and how I always wanted perfect grades just so I could brag to him on weekends. But not all weekends are the same.

On a very random day my sister called me. She had received a call from my dad. Danny was dead.

I have never truly been in touch with my emotions, but that broke me. That hurt me so much. I had barely seen him enough. We never got to exchange phone numbers. I don't even know if he ever liked me back. And now I never will. It has been years and Danny is forgotten. I don't even remember what he looked like anymore. His very beautiful face has washed from my memory. And ever since, I have loved others.

You see, life is so short that we don't even know our deadline. We just know it will come. And for the short while we have here, we tend to spend it pleasing people. We choose our lives and our paths based on what people upload on the internet, the same people who don't know what they want either. We chase validation from strangers, yet when we die, our faces will be forgotten too.

The people closest to us may occasionally remember us when certain moments arise, but life will keep going as it should. Yet we choose our lifestyles based on what will impress these same strangers. People don't have personalities anymore because everyone wants to appear nonchalant. Loving is hard because we can't trust, and when we do trust, people try to scare us out of it based on their own experiences.

Since my very first day on earth I have been taught to fear. Unintentionally, because those who taught me to fear were scared too. If you are from a poor Nigerian background, chances are you were raised religious. You probably went to church every Sunday and feared to question who God was. You feared choosing paths that made you happy because they didn't feel religious enough. I should know, because just like most, my relationship with life has been built on fear.

I cannot count how many times I was told "you will go to hell fire" as a child, that doesn't teach you to love God. It teaches you to be scared of hell. I was told to respect my elders regardless of what they said and never to correct them. They probably meant well, but that taught me to suppress myself more than it taught me to respect others. I was disciplined so strictly that I still flinch now at twenty one years old. These people meant well. Like I said, they too were scared. But of what use is a life built entirely on fear? Why should I live a life I cannot question?

I have read many books and listened to many perspectives over my two decades of being alive. Someone who interests me is Friedrich Nietzsche, a German philosopher, and his idea that morality is a social construct. He believed that what we know as truth is really just opinion passed down through generations. And while that seems unsettling, it made me question so much more. Take something as simple as "do not lie, because it is bad." But what if that lie was going to save a life? Who condemns it as bad, then? Would I be good if I told the truth and someone innocent lost their life because of it?

Albert Camus argued that life has no inherent meaning, but rather than creating meaning, he believed one must embrace the absurdity of existence and continue living in spite of it, Not depressing, actually liberating. And as I revisit the perspectives of men and women who lived before me, I start to see truth outside of everything I was always told to believe.

I miss Danny. And it is absurd that I say this because I barely knew him more than a teenage boy who had big dreams. But I miss him still. Yet this feeling does not stop me from living my life and loving others. Everyone expects me to. He probably does too.

Whether he lived a good life here or not does not matter to anyone now, as they too are concerned about their own lives. Death has never stopped anyone from moving forward.

So while you are here, live life for you. And remember, the only thing we have to fear is fear itself.
